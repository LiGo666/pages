01

Why this exists

I'll be honest. I've been struggling with something for a while, and I haven't found the right words for it. That's why I'm trying a different medium.

I've been watching people speak on behalf of others who never asked to be spoken for. I've been watching relationships between real, complicated humans get translated into the language of power structures until the humans disappear. I've been watching moral purity become a currency — one that buys belonging for some and silence for everyone else. I've been watching a discourse that started with liberation develop a tone that shames, shrinks, and shuts people up — while using the vocabulary of care.

And I've been watching, on the other side, how cynicism, contempt, and raw populism feed on exactly this dynamic. And in between: a silent middle that senses something is off but has no language for it.

I don't know if my reading is right. But I know the experience is shared — by more people than are talking about it. And I believe a burn is one of the few places left where we can explore this without immediately being sorted into camps.

This is not a lecture. I have no thesis. I have questions and a method that lets us explore them with our bodies instead of our arguments.

Not: "Let me tell you what's right."
But: "We're here. What happens between us?"

02

The name

Witness and Withness are one letter apart.

To witness means to observe, to testify, to see from outside. Withness — a term from process philosophy — means being embedded in something, bodily, before thought kicks in. Not knowing about — being in.

A lot of what passes for solidarity right now is witnessing without withness. Seeing without being-with. Testifying without risking anything. The (H) in the title marks this gap. It's a question: Where did the being-with go? And can we get it back — not by talking about it, but by standing in it?

03

What happens

The experience combines two things: theatre sets a scene — you step into it. There is no audience. Everyone is in the room, standing in a position, feeling what it does to them, and then switching.

Step 1 — The scene is set

I lay out a situation in the room — not as a story, but as a set of positions marked by light. Each position has a name. No judgment, no good guys, no bad guys. Just roles in a constellation.

Example: The Affected — someone something happened to. The Voice — someone speaking on their behalf, unasked. The Named — the one being spoken about. The Middle — watching, uncomfortable, silent. The Absent — an empty spot of light: the actual person who disappeared into the abstraction.

Step 2 — You choose a position

Nobody assigns you. You walk to the position that pulls you. That choice already tells you something. Why did you pick The Voice and not The Middle? Why are you avoiding The Named?

Then: silence. You stand there and notice what it feels like. Constellation work has a strange quality — people consistently report that positions carry a felt sense independent of who stands in them. You don't have to believe in anything mystical. Just pay attention.

Step 3 — You switch

This is the core. You move to a different position. If you were The Voice — righteous, certain, speaking truth to power — you become The Named. The one being spoken about. The one reduced to a category.

You don't argue about whether speaking for others is right or wrong. You stand in both positions and feel the difference. Your body gets it before your mind catches up. That's the (H).

Between rounds, we share — briefly, one sentence each. Not discussion, not debate. Just: What did you notice? What surprised you?

04

The scenes

We'll work with two or three constellations. Here's what I'm developing — the final selection depends on the room and the energy:

Scene: "The Voice"

Someone speaks on behalf of someone else who didn't ask. Five positions: The Affected. The Voice. The Named (spoken about, not spoken to). The Silent Middle. The Absent (an empty position — the individual lost in the abstraction).

What it explores: The space between solidarity and appropriation. Between care and control. What does it feel like to be spoken for? What does it feel like to speak and believe you're helping?

Scene: "The Boundary"

Three positions: The Protector — who means well. The Protected — who didn't ask for protection. The Witness — who watches and can't tell if this is love or a cage.

What it explores: When does care become constraint? When does wanting to keep someone safe become wanting to keep them small? And what does the witness owe to either of them?

Scene: "The Mirror"

Four positions: The Movement — certain of its righteousness. The Expelled — who didn't pass the purity test. The Rule — invisible, hovering above everyone. The Old Order — which turns out to look familiar.

What it explores: What happens when a liberation movement builds the same hierarchies it set out to dismantle? Not out of malice — out of not knowing another model. What does it feel like to be expelled by the people who were supposed to free you?

These scenes aren't arguments. They're spaces you walk into. What you find there is yours.

You don't discuss whether speaking for others is right or wrong.
You stand in both positions.
Your body gets it before your mind catches up.

05

The run

Arrival (15 min). You enter one by one. The room is dim — warm light pools mark the positions, still empty. No explanation, no program. Walk the space. Read the positions. Sit down when you're ready.

Scene I (30 min). I introduce the first constellation — briefly, without judgment. You choose a position. Silence. Notice. Then: one sentence each, standing where you stand. What do you feel here? Then: switch. Same scene, different position. Silence. Notice. Share.

Scene II (30 min). A second constellation. Closer to the bone. The room is different now — you're more awake, the silences are heavier, the words more precise. Position. Switch. Share.

Closing (15 min). We come into a circle. No summary, no takeaway, no lesson. Just: What are you leaving with? What surprised you? Speak or be silent. The last silence is the longest. Then we go.

06

What this is not

Not a lecture. I have no thesis. I have observations I can't articulate well enough in words — that's why I'm using bodies and space instead.

Not therapy. There's no healing arc, no resolution. The constellations open something up. What you do with it is your business.

Not a debate. Nobody is asked to defend a position intellectually. You stand in it physically. That's different. It bypasses the part of us that already knows what we think and goes to the part that still has questions.

Not a safe space in the sense of avoiding discomfort. It's a brave space — one where discomfort is the material, not the enemy. You might stand in a position that disgusts you and notice something unexpected. That's the point.

Not an attack on any movement, any politics, any side. The scenes don't have villains. They have humans in positions — and the invitation to find out what each position feels like from the inside.

07

About me

I'm not a theatre director or a trained facilitator. I come from [background]. What drives me toward this form is a simple frustration: when I talk about what I observe in public discourse — the gap between intention and impact, the way moral language can silence instead of liberate — I either get enthusiastic agreement or instant defensiveness. Both miss the point.

I'm looking for a form that goes below the argument. Where nuance is possible. Where you can hold two contradictory things at once without having to choose a side. A burn feels like the right place for this — because you're already outside the default world, already willing to be uncomfortable, already present in a way that's hard to access elsewhere.

This is my first time facilitating this format. I've developed it over months of conversation, reading, and obsession. I know what I want it to do. I don't know if it works. That's why I need a room and people willing to find out with me.

08

What I need

Space
Open floor, ideally enclosed or semi-enclosed. Needs to be dimmable. No chairs, no stage.
Light
Ground-level warm light — candles, lanterns, or dimmable LEDs. No overhead lighting. I'll bring light markers for the positions.
Sound
Silence. Possibly minimal ambient tone. No music. The room sounds like the people in it.
Time slot
90 min. Evening or night preferred — darkness helps. Not adjacent to loud sound camps.

There are no spectators.
You walk into the room, you stand somewhere,
and you find out what that place knows
that you don't.
